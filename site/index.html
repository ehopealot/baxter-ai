What he does

Just ask him. He takes it from there.

There's nothing to learn and no commands to memorize. You write to him the way you'd write to anyone else, and he keeps the thread of it. The things below aren't modes you switch between; he just does all of them.

He shops around for you

Four dishwashers, three insurance quotes, the same flight on six sites. He reads the listings, does the arithmetic on what each one really costs, and tells you which one wins and why.

He does the reading

Point him at the forty-page report, the terms you didn't read, or the argument playing out across a dozen threads. He comes back with the answer rather than a stack of links.

He teaches himself

When he works out how to do something new, he writes himself the procedure and loads it on the next run. A site that fought him last week is routine this week, and he never had to be told twice.

He watches what you care about

The price you're waiting on, the filing that's due, the story you're following. He checks on his own schedule and tells you when something actually happens, instead of you going to look.

He remembers

Baxter keeps notes across every conversation, so you don't re-explain the same context next week. Ask him what you decided back in March and he'll go and look it up.

He keeps the list

Anything bigger than one conversation gets its own living document: what's been decided, and what's still open. He opens and updates it as things move, so keeping the list current is his job rather than yours.

He nudges you

Ask for a reminder and he books it himself in your timezone, once on Thursday or every weekday morning. It turns up whether or not anyone's talking.

He does the busywork

He'll parse a messy spreadsheet, grind through the arithmetic, or turn a column of numbers into a chart. He writes the code, runs it, and the chart or file comes back as an attachment.

Security

The limits live in the code, where he can't argue with them.

Anyone who can message Baxter is writing part of what he reads. A rule written into his prompt is a rule someone can talk him out of, so the ones that matter are enforced where he has no say: the code around him, the container he runs in, or Discord itself.

Your keys never leave
Everything runs on your machine. There's no service in the middle, and no account of ours to sign up for.
He can't read his own keys
Credentials are stripped from every run and written to a file that, on the default setup, nothing he can reach is able to open.
He stays in his own folder
Every file he reads or writes resolves inside his workspace and refuses to escape it.
Code runs with no network
Anything he writes and runs executes in a throwaway container with networking switched off, so there's nowhere to send anything.
Only people you list can email him
The sender list fails closed: leave it empty and he answers no mail at all, from anyone.
He can't message strangers
No run that reads your messages is allowed to open a private Discord conversation. He can reply inside an email thread, but a new email he composes can only go to one address: yours.
He can't moderate on a whim
Deleting anyone else's message works only in channels where you granted the permission. Nothing exists to kick, ban, or hand out roles.
He never answers himself
Baxter ignores his own messages, so he can't talk himself into a loop.
Nothing runs away
An hourly cap per channel and daily ceilings on what he sends bound a runaway exchange that rate limits alone wouldn't.
Scheduled work is bounded
A minimum gap between repeats, a cap on how many tasks exist, and a daily limit on how often they fire.
What he learns can't overwrite
A procedure Baxter writes for himself can never take the place of one that shipped with him.
Self-hosted

He's yours, and he stays that way.

Baxter runs as a handful of small services on a machine you control: a laptop, a home server, a cheap VPS. One command starts them, and they come back up on their own after a restart. There's no account of ours between you and him.

  • Everything he knows lives on a disk you own

    Memory, projects, reminders, and every procedure he's written for himself sit on a named volume. Rebuilds and restarts don't cost him anything he learned, and one command archives the lot or moves him to another machine.

  • He's awake when you aren't

    A scheduler ticks in the background and fires what's due, so a Monday morning digest shows up on Monday morning without anyone opening a laptop.

  • Bring your own model

    He'll run on a hosted model, on Claude, or entirely on a local one where nothing leaves the machine at all. Swapping is one command, and everything he's learned comes with him.

  • You can watch him work

    Every run streams to the logs on your own box: what he was asked, which tools he reached for, what came back. baxter logs shows you, and baxter shell lets you talk to him straight from a terminal.

Setup

Running in four steps.

You'll need Docker with compose v2, make, a model API key, and at least one way to reach him: a Discord application you control, an inbox, or both.

  1. 01

    Configure

    Copy the example environment file and fill in your keys. Every variable is commented in place.

    cp app/.env.example app/.env
  2. 02

    Give him a model

    Any model that supports tool calling will do. The example file already points at a sensible default.

    # app/.env
OPENROUTER_API_KEY=sk-or-...
OPENROUTER_MODEL=openai/gpt-4o
  3. 03

    Pick how to reach him

    For Discord, create an application in the developer portal ↗, enable the Message Content intent, then invite the bot to your server.

    # app/.env
DISCORD_BOT_TOKEN=

    For email, add an AgentMail key and run make inbox once. It creates his inbox and prints the two values to paste back. Then list the addresses allowed to write to him. Leave that empty and he answers nobody.

    # app/.env
AGENTMAIL_API_KEY=
ALLOWED_SENDERS=you@example.com
  4. 04

    Bring him up

    Install the CLI once, then start him. He's live as soon as he connects. Use baxter up mail instead to start the inbox watcher alongside Discord.

    ./install.sh && baxter up

Then, day to day

baxter status
What's running.
baxter logs
Watch what he's doing.
baxter shell
Talk to him from your terminal.
baxter backup
Archive everything he knows.
baxter restore
Roll him back, or move him to another box.
baxter update
Pull, rebuild, restart, in one shot.

Hand him the busywork.
