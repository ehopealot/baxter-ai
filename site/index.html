What he does

Just ask him. He takes it from there.

There's nothing to learn and no commands to memorize. You talk to him the way you'd talk to anyone else, and he keeps the thread of it. The stuff below isn't a menu you pick from. He just does all of it.

He shops around for you

Four dishwashers, three insurance quotes, the same flight on six sites. He reads the listings, works out what each one really costs, and tells you which one's actually worth it, and why.

He does the reading

Point him at the forty-page report, the terms you didn't read, or the argument playing out across a dozen threads. He comes back with the answer, not a stack of links.

He teaches himself

When he works out how to do something new, he writes himself a note and picks it up on the next run. A site that fought him last week is routine this week, and he never has to be told twice.

He watches what you care about

The price you're waiting on, the filing that's due, the story you're following. He checks on his own schedule and pings you when something actually happens, instead of you going to look.

He remembers

Baxter keeps notes across every conversation, so you're not re-explaining the same stuff next week. Ask him what you decided back in March and he'll go and look it up.

He keeps the list

Anything bigger than one conversation gets its own living document: what's been decided, and what's still open. He opens and updates it as things move, so keeping it current is on him, not you.

He nudges you

Ask for a reminder and he books it himself in your timezone, once on Thursday or every weekday morning. It turns up whether or not anyone's talking.

He does the busywork

He'll parse a messy spreadsheet, grind through the arithmetic, or turn a column of numbers into a chart. He writes the code, runs it, and the chart or file comes back as an attachment.

Security

Secure by design.

Anyone who can message Baxter is writing part of what he reads, so the rules that matter aren't ones he can be talked out of. They're enforced by the code around him, the container he runs in, or Discord itself.

Your keys never leave
Everything runs on your machine. There's no middleman, and no account to sign up for.
He doesn't need your logins
Baxter has his own bot and his own inbox, so putting him on Discord and email never means handing over your own account login.
He can't read his own keys
On the default setup, his keys sit somewhere he simply can't open, so a hijacked run has nothing to leak.
He stays in his own folder
He can only read and write files inside his own workspace, nowhere else.
You choose who can email him
You pick which addresses are allowed. Leave the list empty and he replies to no one.
He can't message strangers
He can't start a private message with anyone, and any new email he sends can only go to you.
Self-hosted

He's yours, and he stays that way.

Baxter runs as a handful of small services on a machine you control: a laptop, a home server, a cheap VPS. One command starts them, and they pick themselves back up after a restart. You never have to go through us to reach him.

  • Everything he knows lives on a disk you own

    Memory, projects, reminders, and every procedure he's written for himself sit on a named volume. Rebuilds and restarts don't cost him anything he learned, and one command archives the lot or moves him to another machine.

  • He's awake when you aren't

    A scheduler ticks in the background and fires what's due, so a Monday morning digest shows up on Monday morning without anyone opening a laptop.

  • Bring your own model

    He'll run on a hosted model, on Claude, or entirely on a local one where nothing leaves the machine at all. Swapping is one command, and everything he's learned comes with him.

  • You can watch him work

    Every run streams to the logs on your own box: what he was asked, which tools he reached for, what came back. baxter logs shows you, and baxter shell lets you talk to him straight from a terminal.

Setup

Get up and running.

You'll need Docker with compose v2, make, a model API key, and at least one way to reach him: a Discord application you control, an inbox, or both.

  1. 01

    Configure

    Copy the example environment file and fill in your keys. Every variable is commented in place.

    cp app/.env.example app/.env
  2. 02

    Give him a model

    Any model that can call tools will do. The example file already points at a sensible one.

    # app/.env
OPENROUTER_API_KEY=sk-or-...
OPENROUTER_MODEL=openai/gpt-4o
  3. 03

    Pick how to reach him

    For Discord, create an application in the developer portal ↗, enable the Message Content intent, then invite the bot to your server.

    # app/.env
DISCORD_BOT_TOKEN=

    For email, add an AgentMail key and run make inbox once. It creates his inbox and prints the two values to paste back. Then list the addresses allowed to write to him. Leave that empty and he answers nobody.

    # app/.env
AGENTMAIL_API_KEY=
ALLOWED_SENDERS=you@example.com
  4. 04

    Bring him up

    Install the CLI once, then start him. He's live as soon as he connects. Use baxter up mail instead to start the inbox watcher alongside Discord.

    ./install.sh && baxter up
